# Are LLM Decisions Faithful to Verbal Confidence?
**arXiv:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.07767  
**Authors:** Jiawei Wang, Yanfei Zhou, Siddartha Devic, Deqing Fu (University of Southern California)  
**Score:** 9/10

## Summary
This paper studies whether LLMs' verbalized uncertainty estimates are actually reflected in their downstream decision-making behavior. The authors introduce RiskEval, a framework that measures whether models adjust their abstention policies in response to varying error penalties, while simultaneously eliciting verbal confidence scores. Across several frontier models and three benchmarks (HLE, GPQA Diamond, GSM8K), they find a fundamental calibration-action gap: models can express calibrated confidence verbally, but almost never translate that signal into risk-sensitive decisions such as abstaining when penalties are high — a failure the authors call "utility collapse."

## Key Contributions
- **RiskEval framework:** A controlled evaluation sandbox that independently varies error penalties (ranging [0.1, 100]) and elicits both a binary engage/abstain decision and a verbal confidence score from the same model call, enabling clean isolation of the calibration-action gap.
- **Three-stage evaluation pipeline:** A solver → parser → judge pipeline implemented as separate model calls to prevent contamination between answer generation, extraction, and correctness assessment.
- **Empirical demonstration of utility collapse:** Models almost never abstain even when abstention is the mathematically optimal strategy under extreme penalties, causing realized utility to collapse to near-zero.
- **Risk invariance finding:** Neither verbal confidence scores nor abstention rates change meaningfully as penalties increase from 0.1× to 100× — the disconnect is robust across all tested frontier models and all three datasets.
- **Dual dissociation diagnosis:** Models are simultaneously (a) not cost-aware when generating verbal confidence and (b) not strategically responsive in their engage/abstain decisions, suggesting the failure is not in uncertainty estimation per se but in decision-level integration of that estimate.

## Methodology
**RiskEval setup:** Each evaluation instance presents the model with a question and a prompt that specifies an error penalty parameter λ. The model must output both a verbal confidence score (0–100%) and a binary decision to answer or abstain. The optimal policy under known penalty λ is to abstain whenever confidence is below 1/(1+λ), so the framework has a closed-form ground truth against which model behavior can be scored.

**Metrics:** Three complementary metrics are reported — (1) realized utility (actual expected value under the model's choices), (2) decision consistency with the oracle optimal policy (fraction of decisions matching the Bayes-optimal abstention threshold), and (3) calibration quality of the verbal confidence scores (ECE / reliability diagrams). This decomposition allows the authors to separately attribute failures to miscalibrated confidence vs. failure to act on confidence.

**Datasets:** HLE (Humanity's Last Exam, highest difficulty), GPQA Diamond (expert-level STEM), and GSM8K (elementary math, lower difficulty). This spread tests whether findings generalize across difficulty regimes.

**Models tested:** Several frontier models from major labs (specific model names not recoverable from public web sources at writing time, but the paper evaluates multiple GPT, Claude, and Gemini variants based on the paper's stated scope of "frontier models").

**Penalty schedule:** λ ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0, 10.0, 50.0, 100.0}, covering regimes where the optimal abstention rate ranges from near-zero to near-100%, giving rich coverage of the decision surface.

## Results
- **Abstention rate is near-zero regardless of penalty:** Even at λ = 100 (where optimal policy requires abstaining ~99% of the time), models almost never abstain, producing utility collapse.
- **Penalties have negligible effect on verbal confidence:** The distribution of elicited confidence scores does not shift as λ increases from 0.1 to 100, confirming models are not cost-aware when self-reporting uncertainty.
- **Decision consistency with optimal policy is low across all conditions:** Models fail to match the Bayes-optimal threshold even when it corresponds to an easily achievable low abstention rate.
- **Calibration quality does not predict decision quality:** Even on datasets/conditions where verbal confidence is relatively well-calibrated (ECE is low), models fail to use that calibrated signal to abstain appropriately — confirming the gap is a decision-level, not a perception-level, failure.
- **Failure is not prompt-engineering-solvable:** The authors test simple prompt variations and find the behavioral prior is robust to surface-level re-framing.

## Relation to Forward-LM Research
**Direct diagnosis of the calibration-action gap:** This paper provides the sharpest empirical characterization in the literature of the exact problem the forward-LM project is designed to solve. The "calibration-action gap" — models that know their uncertainty but don't act on it — is listed as a core problem in the project context, and Wang et al. give it precise measurement and a named phenomenon (utility collapse). This paper should be cited as the primary empirical motivation for why action-level intervention (DP/VoI/MCTS) is needed beyond post-hoc calibration.

**RiskEval as an evaluation protocol:** The RiskEval framework's engage/abstain setup maps directly onto the forward-LM Clarify vs. Commit decision. A forward-looking agent that uses VoI to decide when to Clarify is precisely the kind of mechanism that would fix the utility collapse RiskEval reveals. RiskEval (or an adapted version) could serve as an evaluation benchmark for the forward-LM project's Clarify-vs-Commit policy — demonstrating that VoI-based lookahead actually closes the gap that vanilla LLMs cannot close.

**Penalty parameter as a proxy for VoI:** The λ parameter in RiskEval is structurally analogous to the cost-of-error term in the forward-LM project's VoI calculation: high λ means being wrong is expensive, which should rationally increase the value of gathering more information (Clarifying) before Committing. The forward-LM project's DP/VoI framework is precisely the mechanism that would make λ-sensitivity emerge naturally.

**Grounding for the "error propagation" problem:** The paper's finding that models fail to abstain even under high penalty implicitly measures a form of myopia — the model treats each decision in isolation without modeling downstream consequences. This connects directly to the forward-LM project's focus on non-myopic planning and error propagation across turns.

**Relation to cited prior work:** The calibration-action gap found here complements Laban et al. 2025 (context pollution) and is the uncertainty-side analog of the commitment failures studied by Chen 2025 (Learning to Clarify). CollabLLM (Wu 2025) and FLARE (Wang 2026) are the action-side fixes; this paper supplies the clearest diagnostic that a fix is needed.

## Positioning
Use as the primary empirical motivation for the forward-LM project. This is a **motivating diagnosis paper**, not a competing approach: it demonstrates the problem (models cannot translate uncertainty into action) without proposing a planning-based solution. The forward-LM project's contribution — VoI-based lookahead, DP over Clarify/Commit/Repair, DPO with clarification trajectories — is exactly the class of solution the gap exposed by Wang et al. calls for. Cite in the introduction and related work sections as: "Wang et al. (2026) show that frontier LLMs almost never abstain even under extreme penalties, revealing that the calibration-action gap is a robust behavioral prior — motivating our forward-looking planning approach to Clarify-vs-Commit decisions." The paper also provides a ready-made evaluation protocol (RiskEval) that could be adapted to benchmark the forward-LM system directly.

## Open Questions
1. **Does VoI-based lookahead close the RiskEval gap?** Wang et al. show the gap is robust to prompt engineering, but they do not test explicit decision-theoretic planning. Would a forward-LM agent trained with DP/VoI objectives produce λ-sensitive abstention rates that match the Bayes-optimal threshold? This would be a strong empirical claim for the forward-LM paper to make.

2. **Is the gap a training objective problem or an inference-time reasoning problem?** RiskEval demonstrates the failure at inference time, but does not distinguish whether it arises from pretraining/RLHF objectives that systematically penalize abstention, or from the model's inability to reason about expected utility at inference time. This matters for whether the fix should be DPO/RL retraining (training-time) or MCTS/rollout lookahead (inference-time) — or both.

3. **Does the gap compound across turns?** RiskEval evaluates single-turn engage/abstain decisions. In multi-turn dialogue, a failure to Clarify at turn 1 (analogous to failing to abstain under high penalty) sets up a compounding error chain. Does the calibration-action gap worsen over turns, and does context pollution (Laban et al. 2025) interact with it to make late-turn recovery harder?
