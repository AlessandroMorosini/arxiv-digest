# Agentic Uncertainty Quantification
**arXiv:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.15703  
**Authors:** Jiaxin Zhang, Prafulla Kumar Choubey, Shubham Toshniwal, Caiming Xiong, Chien-Sheng Wu  
**Score:** 9/10

## Summary
This paper introduces the Dual-Process Agentic UQ (AUQ) framework, which tackles the "Spiral of Hallucination" problem in long-horizon LLM agents — the phenomenon where early epistemic errors compound irreversibly as they get encoded into the agent's action history and become environmental constraints for subsequent steps. AUQ formalizes two complementary sub-problems: Forward Uncertainty Propagation (preventing errors from solidifying into context) and Inverse Uncertainty Calibration (resolving uncertainty through targeted inference-time reflection). The result is a training-free framework that achieves both better task performance and trajectory-level calibration across closed-loop and open-ended research benchmarks.

## Key Contributions
- **Formal decomposition of agentic UQ** into two orthogonal problems — Forward Propagation (UAM) and Inverse Calibration (UAR) — bridging the gap between passive uncertainty diagnosis and active uncertainty control.
- **System 1 (UAM — Uncertainty-Aware Memory):** A fast, implicit mechanism that stores verbalized confidence scores and semantic explanations in the agent's context window, propagating epistemic state across turns to prevent blind commitment to uncertain prior actions.
- **System 2 (UAR — Uncertainty-Aware Reflection):** A slow, conditional mechanism that uses accumulated uncertainty explanations as rational cues to trigger targeted re-reasoning only when aggregate trajectory confidence falls below a reliability threshold, avoiding the aimless over-correction of naive self-reflection.
- **Trajectory-Level Calibration metrics** (Trajectory-ECE, Trajectory Brier Score, Trajectory AUROC) that aggregate step-wise confidence across a full episode to measure end-state reliability — a new evaluation paradigm for agentic systems.
- **Training-free deployment** using verbalized uncertainty (numeric confidence + natural language rationale) as the core signal, making it model-agnostic and plug-and-play on top of existing ReAct-style agents.

## Methodology
**Core insight:** Verbalized uncertainty (asking the model to emit a confidence score and explanation alongside each action) provides a robust, semantically rich signal that can serve dual purposes — as a passive memory tag (UAM) and as an active trigger for reflection (UAR).

**UAM (System 1 — Fast Path):**
- At each agent step, the model produces an action accompanied by a verbalized confidence score and a brief explanation of its uncertainty.
- Both score and explanation are appended to the agent's context window (memory), forming a running epistemic log.
- Future steps attend to this log, implicitly conditioning subsequent decisions on accumulated doubt without requiring explicit re-reasoning.
- Mathematically this implements Forward Uncertainty Propagation: uncertainty from step *t* is carried as a constraint into the policy at step *t+k*.

**UAR (System 2 — Slow Path):**
- An aggregate trajectory confidence is computed from the running uncertainty scores (e.g., product or minimum over recent steps).
- When this aggregate drops below a predefined reliability threshold, UAR is triggered.
- UAR uses the stored semantic explanations as rational cues to perform a targeted reflection: it re-examines the specific uncertain decision points rather than redoing the entire trajectory.
- A Memory Expansion Mechanism handles persistence: if the aggregate score remains low after initial reflection, the agent expands its working memory and re-executes reflection.
- UAR implements Inverse Uncertainty Calibration: inference-time compute is conditionally spent only where epistemic risk is highest.

**Integration:** The two systems are complementary — UAM provides always-on epistemic bookkeeping (lowest ECE), while UAR provides targeted correction (lowest Brier Score). Running both yields the best overall calibration-performance tradeoff.

## Results
**ALFWorld (closed-loop household task completion):**
- AUQ: 74.3% success rate vs. ReAct baseline (~63.6%), a +10.7 pp gain.
- Trajectory-ECE: 0.109 (vs. 0.306 for ReAct) — major calibration improvement.
- End-state AUROC: >0.96.

**WebShop (online shopping agent):**
- AUQ: 42.9% success rate vs. ReAct baseline (~29.3%), a +13.6 pp gain.

**DeepResearch Bench (100 PhD-level open-ended research tasks):**
- AUQ Overall Score: 52.09, outperforming the strongest closed-source system baseline (49.71) and best open-source competitor (50.62).
- Advantage driven by superior Insight (54.21) and Comprehensiveness (51.60) sub-scores.

**Calibration ablation:** UAM alone achieves best ECE; UAR alone achieves best Brier Score; Dual-Process (both) achieves the optimal tradeoff. This validates the complementary design.

## Relation to Forward-LM Research
This paper is highly relevant to the forward-LM project across multiple dimensions:

1. **Error propagation across turns** — AUQ directly addresses the forward-LM project's "error propagation across turns" problem. The "Spiral of Hallucination" is essentially the multi-turn analogue of context pollution: a wrong answer at turn *t* becomes a false premise at turn *t+1*. UAM's approach of tagging each output with explicit epistemic metadata is directly applicable to dialogue state tracking.

2. **Calibration-action gap** — The project identifies the calibration-action gap (models know their uncertainty but don't act on it) as a key problem. AUQ is the most direct published solution: it provides the engineering pattern for converting a verbalized confidence score into a conditional control signal (trigger reflection vs. proceed). The UAR threshold mechanism is a concrete, deployable instance of the Clarify-vs-Commit-vs-Repair decision.

3. **When to Repair** — The UAR trigger (aggregate trajectory confidence < threshold → reflect) maps almost directly onto the forward-LM project's "Repair" action branch. The paper provides empirical evidence that threshold-based conditional repair outperforms always-reflect (Reflexion) and never-reflect (ReAct) baselines — directly supporting the project's hypothesis about selective intervention.

4. **Relation to VoI/DP methods** — AUQ uses a heuristic threshold rather than a principled Value-of-Information calculation. The forward-LM project's DP/VoI framework could replace or augment this threshold with a theoretically grounded decision, potentially improving over AUQ's fixed-threshold design.

5. **CollabLLM / Clarify connection** — AUQ focuses on the Repair branch; it does not model the Clarify vs. Answer distinction that CollabLLM and Learning to Clarify address. The forward-LM project's contribution sits in the Clarify decision space, where AUQ provides orthogonal but complementary machinery.

6. **Verbalized confidence as dialogue signal** — UAM's technique of propagating verbalized confidence through context is directly usable in multi-turn dialogue to maintain a running epistemic state about the current user request, which is relevant for the project's non-myopic dialogue agents.

## Positioning
**Primary positioning: Closely related work + partial baseline.**

- **As related work:** Cite AUQ in the section on uncertainty-aware agents and the calibration-action gap. It is the strongest published example of closing the gap between UQ diagnosis and active control in agentic settings.
- **As a baseline:** AUQ's UAR threshold-based Repair mechanism is a natural baseline for the project's DP/VoI-based action selection. The project can compare its principled VoI-driven Clarify/Repair policy against AUQ's heuristic threshold.
- **As motivating evidence:** The paper's "Spiral of Hallucination" framing and the trajectory-level calibration metrics provide strong empirical motivation for why forward-looking error prevention matters — useful for the project's introduction.
- **Not a direct competitor:** AUQ does not address the Clarify-vs-Commit distinction in dialogue, does not use DP/MCTS planning, and is not trained with DPO. The forward-LM project's contribution is differentiated.

## Open Questions
1. **Fixed threshold vs. learned/principled trigger:** AUQ uses a static confidence threshold to trigger UAR. How much performance is left on the table compared to a VoI-based decision that accounts for the expected cost of repair vs. the expected cost of propagating the error? Can the project's DP framework produce a better trigger policy?

2. **Clarify vs. Repair distinction:** AUQ only implements the Repair branch — it corrects uncertainty after committing to an action. In multi-turn dialogue, the more valuable (and cheaper) intervention is often to Clarify *before* committing. Does propagating UAM-style epistemic tags through dialogue history provide enough signal to also drive a Clarify decision, or does that require a separate model of user intent uncertainty?

3. **Verbalized confidence reliability at scale:** UAM relies on the model accurately verbalizing its own confidence. At what failure modes does verbalized confidence diverge from actual epistemic state (e.g., confidently wrong reasoning chains)? How does the project's trajectory rollout approach handle situations where the model is miscalibrated in its self-assessment — i.e., when the signal UAM relies on is itself untrustworthy?
